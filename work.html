research

may 2017 — august 2017

Stanford Vision and Neuro-Development Lab w/ PI Anthony Norcia +
Stanford Medicine, Ophthalmology w/ PI Jeffrey Goldberg, MD/PhD ↓↓↓

Recent animal models have determined that a specific subset of retinal ganglion cells are the first to die in glaucoma. Thus, if we can develop a set of visual stimuli that optimally stimulates that subset and record resultant signals from visual cortex, we can then use those stimuli in an early screening for glaucoma.

With each iteration of the stimulus set, I recorded human subjects' cortical responses via high-density electroencephalography (128-channel EGI). I then wrote MATLAB scripts to analyze the data in both the time and frequency domains, searching for a difference in the responses of the ON and OFF magnocellular pathways. Various techniques/tools/tests used include Reliable Components Analysis for dimensionality reduction, error calculation with eigenvalue decomposition, & paired T-tests.

may 2016 — august 2016

Stanford Vision and Neuro-Development Lab w/ PI Anthony Norcia ↓↓↓

Certain animated stimuli are bistable – when watching the stimulus, perception unpredictably and frequently reverses between two different percepts of motion. I studied a bistable stimulus that alternated between flicker (non-moving flashing) and coherent motion (moving back and forth between two locations). The brain region that differs between these percepts is implicated in decision-making, or, in a perhaps more ambitious claim, consciousness.

I used a two-session paradigm: psychophysics and high-density EEG (128-channel EGI). Psychophysics was two-alternative forced choice, where the subject reported percept switching during presentation of various stimuli. My MATLAB script took this behavioral data and fitted a custom Weibull psychometric curve to determine the ideal stimulus parameters for 50% flicker and 50% apparent motion. This ideal stimulus was then presented in the EEG half. I then analyzed this data in the time domain and found the cortical regions where response differed most between percepts.
